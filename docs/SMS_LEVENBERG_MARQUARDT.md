# Levenberg-Marquardt damping for the SMS Newton solver

**Captured:** 2026-05-03 on the displaced Veach egg with the user's
updated camera (278, 450, -650 lookat (278, 250, 278)) and the
`disp_scale 0..10` sweep at 100×100 / 4 spp / `sms_seeding "snell"` /
`sms_multi_trials 1` / `sms_two_stage FALSE` / no photons (apples-to-
apples isolation of NewtonSolve behaviour).

## Why

The displacement-induced power drop on heavy-displacement glass caustics
([SMS_PHOTON_DECOUPLING_AND_DISPLACEMENT_LIMIT.md](SMS_PHOTON_DECOUPLING_AND_DISPLACEMENT_LIMIT.md))
was attributed to plain Newton plateau-stalling: the residual `||C||`
parks at ~[0.1, 1.0) for 73-85 % of failures, neither close enough to
accept (`< 1e-2`) nor diverging away.  Iteration budget did nothing
(`maxIter=200` identical to `maxIter=30`), and the existing
backtracking line search already shrinks oversize Newton steps to the
same end point a step-norm cap would (verified: cap fires 76 % of
iters at disp=0 with avg 262× oversize raw step, but cap-vs-no-cap
Newton-fail rate identical within MC noise).

That diagnostic implicated **step direction**, not step size, as the
bottleneck.  The textbook fix is Levenberg-Marquardt damping, which
blends Newton with steepest descent via a damping parameter λ:

- λ → 0: pure Newton (quadratic convergence near a root)
- λ → ∞: small gradient-descent steps (escapes plateaus where Newton's
  J⁻¹·C is unreliable)

LM adapts λ between iterations:
- accepted line-search step → shrink λ (back toward Newton)
- rejected line-search step → grow λ (more gradient-descent-like)

## What

**Variant: damped Newton, not full LM normal equations.**  We damp
`J`'s diagonal in place by `λ × mean(|J_ii|)` and reuse the existing
block-tridiagonal solver.  Full Marquardt-style `(JᵀJ + λ·diag(JᵀJ))Δ
= JᵀC` would double the bandwidth (block-tridiag squared = block-
pentadiag) and need a different solver.  This damped-Newton form
captures the algorithmic benefit (escape from ill-conditioned
directions) while preserving the existing solver path.

Implementation in [ManifoldSolver.cpp](../src/Library/Utilities/ManifoldSolver.cpp):

- `ManifoldSolverConfig::useLevenbergMarquardt` (bool, **default false
  — opt-in**) — runtime toggle.  LM is ~50-100% slower than the legacy
  pure-Newton + line-search path on heavy-displacement scenes (where
  escalation iterations consume the iter budget) for ~5pp Newton-fail
  improvement; off by default because that ratio doesn't justify the
  cost as a global default.  Set true at API construction time
  (`smsConfig.useLevenbergMarquardt = true` in
  [SMSConfig.h](../src/Library/Utilities/SMSConfig.h) flowing through
  [RISE_API.cpp](../src/Library/RISE_API.cpp)) for the heavy-displaced
  mesh scenes that benefit.  No scene-language plumbing yet;
  callers override the default via the API.
- `kLM_LambdaInit = 1e-3`, `LambdaMin = 1e-9`, `LambdaMax = 1e9`.
- `kLM_LambdaUp = 10.0`, `LambdaDown = 0.1`.
- λ is **per-Solve-call** (local stack variable in `NewtonSolve`),
  starting at 0 each call so the first iter is pure Newton.  No global
  state, no thread races.  When the runtime toggle is false, λ stays
  0 throughout and every LM-related branch is a no-op.

The damping is added to each diagonal block's (0,0) and (1,1) entries
right after `BuildJacobian` returns and before `SolveBlockTridiagonal`
is called.  Scaling by `mean(|J_ii|)` makes the damping order-of-
magnitude appropriate regardless of chain length / IOR / surface
scale — raw `λ·I` would be dominated by the original `J` at
well-conditioned vertices and have no effect, while at ill-conditioned
vertices a large enough scaled λ moves the step direction toward
gradient descent.

Existing infrastructure preserved:
- 10-attempt backtracking line search runs after the damped solve as
  before.
- Soft-convergence acceptance at `10 × solverThreshold` after iter
  budget exhausted.
- Curvature-aware Jacobian (`includeCurvature=true`) on by default.
- Per-iter chain restoration on rejected step.

The only behavioural change at the failure path:
**before**, `if (!improved) return false;` (or soft-converge);
**after**, when LM is active and `!improved`, escalate λ and `continue`
to the next iter slot.  When λ exceeds `LambdaMax` without progress,
fall through to the legacy fail path (the problem is genuinely beyond
LM's reach — multi-basin landscape with no descent connection).

## Cost

Per-Solve work is dominated by `BuildJacobian` (surface derivatives)
+ `SolveBlockTridiagonal` (cheap) + `UpdateVertexOnSurface` (medium).
LM adds zero per-iter cost beyond the diagonal damping (~2k floating
adds per iter), but **escalation iterations** count against the
`maxIterations` budget.

Measured on the production scene (`mlt_veach_egg_pt_sms_displaced.RISEscene`,
400×400, M=16, two_stage=TRUE, disp_scale=4, 4 spp):

- 5.08 M Solve calls
- 170.9 M Newton iterations (avg 33.7 iters/call, with `maxIter=30`
  per-iter budget — most calls don't max out, but tail does)
- LM rescued 498,203 chains
- 36.7% of iters had `λ > 0` applied; 9.1% of iters were escalations

Net runtime impact ~50–100% slower on heavy-displacement scenes.
On smooth scenes the escalation rate is much lower (k1_refract: 65%
of iters had `λ > 0` but only 0.0004% of escalations succeed —
mostly LM is a small constant overhead).  Negligible on scenes with
no SMS.

## When LM doesn't help

The ~50% Newton-fail floor on heavy displacement remains: even with
LM, disp=10 sees 67% of solves fail.  Newton — pure or damped — can't
fix two regimes:

1. **Multi-basin landscape with no descent connection from the seed.**
   LM's gradient-descent direction is local to the current iterate;
   if the right basin is on the other side of a saddle, no first-
   order method finds it.  The tool is **better seeding** (photon
   seeds in the right basin), not a better solver.
2. **C¹ discontinuities at triangle edges.**  Our `BuildJacobian`
   returns zero curvature across mesh edges, which gives the right
   answer for the within-triangle case but produces a Jacobian that
   doesn't model the actual surface near edges.  LM doesn't fix
   wrong J; it only damps it.  The tool is **smoother surface
   evaluation** (analytic primitives, or smoothing operators on the
   mesh).

Both are flagged for further work in
[SMS_PHOTON_DECOUPLING_AND_DISPLACEMENT_LIMIT.md](SMS_PHOTON_DECOUPLING_AND_DISPLACEMENT_LIMIT.md)
under "Three avenues that would actually move the disp=10 number".

## Follow-up investigations (2026-05-03 evening)

After LM landed, four more experiments tested specific hypotheses about
the source of the residual mesh penalty.  All are reproducible by
flipping the corresponding compile-time gate (default 0 / off in
`ManifoldSolver.cpp`).

### Step-norm cap (`kNewtonStepNormCapFrac`) — null result

Bound each Newton iter's max world-space vertex displacement to
`<frac> × mean(inter-vertex segment length)` so an ill-conditioned
Jacobian doesn't fling a vertex far off the surface.  Mitsuba's reference
applies an analogous tangent-space cap.

Measured at disp=10 with `kNewtonStepNormCapFrac = 0.25`:
the cap fires 76 % of iters on raw Newton steps that are on average
**262× larger than the cap**, but the existing 10-halving line search
already shrinks those steps to the same end point a cap would.
Newton-fail rate is unchanged within MC noise (23.1 % → 23.5 %).

Verdict: **the cap is redundant with the line search.**  Code retained
as inactive instrumentation.

### Edge-aware Newton step (`SMS_EDGE_AWARE_NEWTON`) — slightly negative

Within one triangle, Phong-interpolated normals are linear in
barycentric coords, so the Jacobian's predicted normal change matches
the actual rotation.  Across a triangle edge, the linearization
changes — predicted vs actual diverge.  The check rejects steps where
actual rotation is more than `3×` the predicted rotation (with a 0.05
absolute floor so the test doesn't fire at low-curvature vertices).

Measured: rejection rate climbs cleanly with displacement (0 % at
disp=0 → 12 % at disp=10), confirming the check correctly identifies
"untrusted linearization."  But ok rate **drops** by 0.0–0.4 pp at
every disp value.  The line search's `||C||`-decrease test was already
accepting cross-edge steps that did decrease residual; pre-rejecting
on linearization-trust just blocks useful moves.

Verdict: **the C¹ defect doesn't manifest as "Newton overshoots into
bad territory."**  It manifests as "Newton's predicted descent direction
is noisy across edges, but the move it actually makes is still useful
on average."  Code retained as inactive instrumentation.

### Smooth-base Jacobian throughout (`SMS_SMOOTH_BASE_JACOBIAN`) — partial gain

Use the underlying analytical surface (the smooth ellipsoid) for
`dpdu/dpdv/dndu/dndv` while keeping the bumpy mesh position and normal.
Newton's descent direction comes from a C∞ smooth curvature model,
while the residual `C` is still evaluated against real bumpy normals.

Measured on the disp 0..10 sweep:
- ok % gain: +0.0 to +4.1 pp (best at mid-disp where smooth curvature
  is still a decent local approximation).
- Newton-fail rate at high-disp **increases** (e.g. disp=10: 72.0 %
  → 75.4 %) but physics-fail decreases (4.9 % → 1.2 %), so net ok %
  is slightly higher.
- Energy ratios: mostly higher, no collapse (contrary to the prior
  recollection that "energy collapsed").

Verdict: **partial improvement, not the silver bullet.**  Caps out at
~4 pp recovery (vs the 9 pp diagnostic gap) because the position-
mesh-vs-J-smooth mismatch limits the gain.  Code retained as inactive
instrumentation; could compose with LM in a future session.

### dpdu/dpdv projection onto shading-N tangent plane (`MESH_PROJECT_DERIVATIVES_TO_TANGENT_PLANE`) — null result

`TriangleMeshGeometryIndexedSpecializations.h` projects the UV-inverted
`dpdu/dpdv` onto the shading-normal tangent plane, dropping any
component along the shading normal.  pbrt-v4 and Mitsuba 3 both emit
**raw** UV-inverted derivatives without this projection (verified by
direct source comparison).  RISE's projection is a one-liner deviation.

Hypothesis tested: SMS's `UpdateVertexOnSurface` predicts new world
position via `position + dpdu·du + dpdv·dv`.  If `dpdu` has its
shading-N component dropped, the prediction is off in that direction.

Measured at disp 0..10, projection ON (current default) vs OFF
(matching pbrt/Mitsuba): **Newton-fail identical to within 0.1 pp at
every disp value.**  Energy ratio swings ±0.1 with no consistent
direction (MC noise).

Verdict: **the projection is a benign deviation, not a bug.**  Newton's
J uses `dpdu/dpdv` mostly through projected quantities (cross products
in the half-vector residual) where the dropped N-component doesn't
matter.  The position-prediction discrepancy in `UpdateVertexOnSurface`
exists in theory but is small and absorbed by the ray-cast re-snap.
Code retained as instrumentation toggle so future work can re-test if
the math comparison comes up again.

### Tessellation-detail sweep — REFUTES the mesh-discretization hypothesis

Hypothesis: the 9 pp gap between "smooth ellipsoid" and "tessellated
mesh at disp=0" is secant-vs-tangent error that fades as detail
increases.  Test: render at `detail` ∈ {32, 64, 128, 256, 512} (16×
range) at both disp=0 and disp=10.

**Result at disp=0** — Newton-fail rate is **invariant to mesh detail**:

| detail | ok% | newt% |
|--------|----:|------:|
| 32     | 67.6% | 22.9% |
| 128    | 67.2% | 23.2% |
| 512    | 67.2% | 23.2% |
| (smooth analytical reference) | 75.9% | 14.5% |

Even at detail=512, where mesh vertices are essentially coincident
with the smooth ellipsoid surface, Newton-fail stays at 23.2 %.  The
gap to analytical (8.7 pp) does NOT close.

**The 9 pp gap is structural, not discretization-scaling.**  It comes
from the mesh-derivative path computing interpolated quantities while
the analytical path computes exact ones — and is independent of how
good the mesh's approximation is.

**Result at disp=10** — Newton-fail rate **INCREASES** with finer
mesh:

| detail | ok% | newt% | ratio |
|--------|----:|------:|------:|
| 32     | 35.5% | 60.0% | 0.69  |
| 128    | 23.1% | 72.1% | 0.37  |
| 512    | 21.9% | 73.3% | 0.23  |

Counter-intuitive but consistent: the displacement function
(`perlin2d_painter` with octaves=4, scale=8.0) has high-frequency
content.  Coarse tessellation **aliases** the high octaves away,
producing a smoother surface than the displacement function actually
defines.  Fine tessellation samples those high octaves correctly,
producing more per-triangle normal jumps — and Newton fundamentally
can't navigate sharp jumps regardless of how its J is computed.

**This refines the C¹ understanding from the LM section above.**  The
problem isn't "the mesh is too coarse to model the surface."  The
problem is "the displacement function carries more high-frequency
content than the solver can track" and "finer mesh exposes that
content rather than hiding it."

### Math-pipeline review vs pbrt-v4 and Mitsuba 3 — RISE is correct

A direct source-level comparison of the triangle-mesh derivative
computation:

|                                       | RISE                | pbrt-v4   | Mitsuba 3        |
|---------------------------------------|---------------------|-----------|------------------|
| dpdu/dpdv UV-matrix inversion         | ✓ correct           | ✓ correct | ✓ correct        |
| dndu/dndv parameterization            | UV (matrix-inv)     | UV        | barycentric      |
| Chain-rule `(I−nnᵀ)/\|Nraw\|` on dndu/dv | **✓ correct**     | omitted   | **✓ correct**    |
| dpdu/dpdv projection onto shading-N   | RISE-only (benign)  | not done  | not done         |
| Right-handedness flip vs n            | ✓                   | ✓         | ✓                |

RISE matches Mitsuba on the chain-rule normalization that pbrt actually
omits (Mitsuba is the more rigorous reference for SMS use; pbrt's
`dndu` is the raw interpolant derivative which is technically less
correct for unit-normal derivatives).  The single deviation — dpdu/dpdv
projection — is RISE-only AND measured to have zero effect on
convergence.  No bug found in the math.

### Smooth-displacement function (Gerstner, low-frequency) — ROOT CAUSE PROVEN

The detail-sweep refutation pointed to "high-frequency displacement
content the mesh tessellation can't smooth out" as the source of the
~50 pp displacement-induced power drop.  Final experiment: replace the
Perlin painter (high-frequency content via 4 octaves at scale=8) with a
**Gerstner painter** configured to produce a low-frequency, low-
gradient field:

```
gerstnerwave_painter
{
    name              pnt_egg_bumps
    num_waves         2
    median_wavelength 1.0      // one wavelength fills entire UV [0,1]
    wavelength_range  1.0      // no spread; both waves at same wavelength
    median_amplitude  0.5      // 2 × 0.5 = 1.0 max sum, matching Perlin range
    amplitude_power   0
    wind_dir          1 1
    directional_spread 0
    dispersion_speed  0
    seed              42
    time              0
}
```

Per-UV gradient magnitude: ~6.3 (vs Perlin scale=8 octaves=4 at ~16+).

**Disp 0..10 sweep, Perlin vs smooth Gerstner (LM=0, M=1, no two-stage):**

| disp | Perlin ok% | Smooth ok% | Perlin newt% | Smooth newt% | Perlin ratio | Smooth ratio |
|------|-----------:|-----------:|-------------:|-------------:|-------------:|-------------:|
| 0    | 67.2%      | 67.2%      | 23.1%        | 23.2%        | 0.91         | 0.90         |
| 1    | 55.0%      | **66.3%**  | 38.3%        | 24.7%        | 0.94         | 0.96         |
| 5    | 33.9%      | **57.8%**  | 63.7%        | 29.8%        | 0.66         | 0.91         |
| 10   | 23.1%      | **49.3%**  | 72.0%        | 38.5%        | 0.41         | 0.71         |

ok rate at disp=10: **+26 pp** improvement (49.3 % vs 23.1 %).
Newton-fail rate at disp=10: **half** (38.5 % vs 72.0 %).
Energy ratio at disp=10: **almost 2×** (0.71 vs 0.41).

**Detail-invariance test @ disp=10 — the third smoking-gun signature:**

| detail | Perlin ok% | Smooth ok% |
|--------|-----------:|-----------:|
| 32     | 35.5%      | 48.1%      |
| 64     | 27.2%      | 47.9%      |
| 128    | 23.1%      | 49.3%      |
| 256    | 22.4%      | 50.0%      |
| 512    | 21.9%      | 50.3%      |
| spread (32→512) | **-13.6 pp** | **+2.2 pp** |

**Smooth function is invariant to tessellation; Perlin gets worse with
finer detail.**  This nails the mechanism: finer detail samples Perlin's
high octaves rather than aliasing them away → more per-triangle normal
jumps → Newton plateau-stalls more often.  A smooth function has no
high-frequency content for finer detail to expose, so it's invariant.

## The actual root cause

**The displacement function's spectral content drives SMS Newton failure
on displaced meshes.**  Not the mesh tessellation density.  Not the
derivative computation pipeline.  Not the absence of LM.  The mesh is
the messenger; the cause is "high-frequency displacement content
sampled at vertices produces large C¹ normal-field jumps at triangle
edges, and Newton can't navigate sharp jumps."

Practical recommendations for SMS-on-displaced-meshes scenes:

1. **Prefer low-frequency displacement painters** (Gerstner with long
   wavelengths, smooth-falloff perlin with octaves=1, etc.) over high-
   octave Perlin or Voronoi.  +20-26 pp ok rate at the same visual
   amplitude is a free win.
2. **At a given displacement painter, finer tessellation does NOT help**
   and frequently HURTS.  Coarser is fine — the aliasing was actually
   masking the problem; un-masking it doesn't fix it.
3. **The remaining ~9 pp mesh penalty** (smooth-disp at any detail vs
   analytical reference) is the structural mesh-vs-analytical gap that
   smooth-base Jacobian recovers ~4 pp of.  The other ~5 pp is position
   discretization, only addressable with higher-order surface
   representations.

## Summary of where the disp=10 power drop comes from

Final breakdown after the full investigation arc:

- **~9 pp** structural gap between mesh-interpolated and analytical-
  derivatives at any tessellation detail (not closeable by tessellation).
  ~4 pp recoverable with smooth-base J substitution; ~5 pp is position
  discretization needing higher-order surface representation.
- **~5 pp** Newton plateau-stalling intrinsic to the smooth-displaced-
  ellipsoid problem (recoverable with LM).
- **~40+ pp** **high-frequency-displacement-content-induced Newton
  plateau-stalling**, which is the root cause of the dramatic
  collapse.  Recoverable by either (a) using a smoother displacement
  painter, or (b) at the cost of low-pass-filtering the displacement
  before applying it to the mesh.

Net production state: LM on, all four solver experiments + the
projection toggle compiled out as inactive instrumentation, ready for
future investigation without re-deriving the negative results.  The
final Gerstner-vs-Perlin experiment isn't gated by code (it's a scene
authoring choice) — but the spectral-content principle is captured
here so future scene authors can pick low-frequency painters when
SMS quality matters.
